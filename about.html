the origin

There’s a program at UNC called C-START, standing for Carolina Students Taking Academic Responsibility through teaching. Each year, about 8 or so students are selected to teach a course of their own design to fellow college students. The courses are pass/fail, and enroll 10-15 students with no prerequisite knowledge.

I taught a course in spring 2020 called “Game Show Theory”, which coalesces different topics in physics, economics, mathematics, statistics, and psychology to explain human behavior and optimal strategy in different games.The goals of the course were to discuss the role that game shows have played in depicting and evolving American culture, and to discuss the academic determinants of this role. These academic faculties, in the context of the course, were the core subjects of physics, statistics and game theory.

I took interest in the use of games to teach dense academic material. Randy Pausch, a now deceased computer scientist at Carnegie Mellon, speaks of a similar concept, “head-fake learning," in his seminal Last Lecture. The essence of the term is based in sports, where head-fakes are used to lead opponents in different directions from the one the player intends to go. Randy says: “The best way to teach somebody is to have them think they’re learning something else.”

the research

At the summer 2020 meeting of the Academic Association of Physics Teachers, we delivered a conference presentation titled "Presenting Physics Concepts via Head-Fake Learning." As this signals, our research is in the domain of physics educational mechanisms.
